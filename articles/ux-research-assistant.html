Building a UX Research Assistant

Product Design · Side Project · 2025

Problem UX designers often pick the wrong research method for their sample size and goals
Solution Interactive tool with 2 dropdowns that recommends the right method with step-by-step guidance
Result 6 research categories, 18 scenarios, 24 glossary terms with inline tooltips

Most UX designers know they should do research. Fewer know which method to use when. I partnered with a developer to build a tool that answers one question: "Given my situation and sample size, what research method should I use?"

Project Details

  • Role: Content design, UX strategy, information architecture
  • Collaboration: Partnered with a developer for implementation
  • Timeline: 3 weeks from concept to launch
  • Discovery: 8 interviews with UX writers across B2B, B2C, agencies, and health tech
  • Framework: Based on Nielsen Norman Group research methods

Discovery Research

Before building anything, I interviewed 8 UX writers from different contexts: B2B enterprise, B2C apps, design agencies, and health tech. I wanted to understand how they currently approach research decisions.

Interview Questions

  • When you need to validate a design decision, where do you start?
  • How do you decide which research method to use?
  • What's the last research you ran? How did you choose that approach?
  • What stops you from doing more research?
  • When research goes wrong, what usually caused it?

What I Heard

"I know I should test this, but I genuinely don't know if I need a survey or interviews or a usability test."
— P4, Health Tech
6 of 8 felt paralyzed when starting research
"We ran an A/B test with 8 people and called it a winner. Looking back... yeah, that was wrong."
— P6, B2C E-commerce
Sample size confusion led to false confidence
"I've read all the NN/g articles. I know what a card sort is. I just don't know when to use one instead of a tree test."
— P3, Design Agency
The gap is application, not theory

Design Requirements

The interviews shaped three core requirements:

  1. Start with the question, not the method. Users think "Is this clear?" not "Should I run a comprehension test?"
  2. Make sample size a first-class input. The right method depends heavily on how many users you have.
  3. Bridge theory to action. Don't just name the method—provide steps, warnings, and presentation language.

The Pivot

My first instinct was to build an A/B test calculator. It worked — but it solved the wrong problem. A designer with 8 users doesn't need help interpreting results. They need to know A/B testing isn't the right method for their situation.

I scrapped the calculator. Instead of asking "What were your results?", the tool would ask "What do you need to learn?"

The first version had 3 categories: Is the message clear? Which option feels right? Does it convert? This worked for evaluative research, but I was missing half the picture.

I compared my tool against Nielsen Norman Group's research methods chart. They categorize methods into four phases: Discover, Explore, Test, and Listen. My first version only covered "Test." Methods like user interviews, field studies, card sorting, and journey mapping were completely absent.

The solution: expand from 3 options to 6, phrased as questions a real person would ask — without adding a third dropdown.
